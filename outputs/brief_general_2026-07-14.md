# Daily Macro Intelligence Brief — Emirates Development Bank
**Date:** Tuesday, 14 July 2026
**Prepared by:** Macro Analysis (general market brief)

Today's macro picture is dominated by a fresh escalation in the Middle East that has pushed oil sharply higher, set against stable but cautious central-bank policy in both the US and the UAE. Below is a plain-language summary of the key developments and what they mean for UAE industry.

---

## 1. US Federal Reserve — holding, with a hawkish tilt

The Federal Reserve has kept its benchmark interest rate unchanged at **3.50%–3.75%** through its 2026 meetings, most recently at the 16–17 June meeting under new Chair Kevin Warsh. That statement dropped the earlier bias toward rate cuts and instead flagged the possibility of a rate *increase* later in 2026, citing solid growth and inflation still running above the Fed's 2% target.

The next decision is **29 July**. Markets currently expect the Fed to hold again, but that view hinges on today's inflation data (see below). This is a "non-SEP" meeting, meaning the Fed will not publish updated economic projections or a new "dot plot."

**Watch today:** US June Consumer Price Index (CPI) is released at 8:30 a.m. New York time. Consensus expects headline inflation to ease to roughly **3.9% year-on-year** (from 4.2% in May), helped by a drop in gasoline prices, with core inflation around **2.9%**. A hotter-than-expected reading would revive talk of a July or later rate hike.

## 2. UAE Central Bank (CBUAE) — steady, tracking the Fed

Because the UAE dirham is pegged to the US dollar, the CBUAE moves its policy rate in step with the Federal Reserve. The CBUAE **Base Rate stands at 3.65%** and has been held there since late 2025, mirroring the Fed's hold.

Local interbank borrowing costs remain broadly stable, with the three-month EIBOR (the benchmark for many UAE loans) around the high-3% range. In practice this means UAE borrowers — including businesses on floating-rate loans — are unlikely to see cheaper financing until the Fed itself begins cutting, which is not currently expected in the near term.

*Sources: [CBUAE — Base Rate maintained at 3.65%](https://www.centralbank.ae/media/kc0p2tuf/cbuae-maintains-the-base-rate-at-3-65-en.pdf); [Gulf News — UAE keeps base rate steady after Fed hold](https://gulfnews.com/business/banking/uae-keeps-base-rate-steady-after-federal-reserve-leaves-rates-unchanged-1.500578304).*

## 3. Oil prices — sharp jump on Strait of Hormuz tensions

Oil has rallied hard this week on renewed conflict around the Strait of Hormuz, the waterway that carries roughly a fifth of the world's seaborne oil.

- **Brent crude** is trading near **$87 per barrel** (about $86.99 spot on the morning of 14 July), up more than 10% on the week and at a one-month high.
- **WTI (US benchmark)** is around **$79–80 per barrel**.

The driver is geopolitical: the US President announced a **20% fee on all cargo transiting the Strait of Hormuz** and reinstated a blockade on Iranian vessels, raising fears of supply disruption. The situation is fast-moving and headlines are mixed — some reports point to escalation, others to on-and-off de-escalation talks — so prices are volatile and could swing several dollars a barrel on any new development.

## 4. UAE economic conditions — solid growth, low inflation

The UAE economy remains one of the strongest performers in the region:

- **Growth:** The IMF projects real GDP growth of about **5% in 2026**, and the CBUAE's own forecast is even higher at around **5.6%**. Growth is broad-based — supported both by higher oil output and by a resilient non-oil economy.
- **Diversification:** Non-oil sectors now make up roughly **80% of GDP**, led by financial services, manufacturing, construction, trade and tourism. This is the core of the UAE's long-term strategy to reduce dependence on hydrocarbons.
- **Inflation:** UAE consumer inflation is low and stable (running in the low single digits), well below levels seen in the US and Europe.

Higher oil prices, while disruptive globally, are a net positive for UAE government revenue, giving the state more fiscal room to invest in infrastructure and industrial projects.

*Sources: [IMF — United Arab Emirates](https://www.imf.org/en/countries/are); [Gulf News — UAE economy to grow 5.6% in 2026](https://gulfnews.com/business/banking/uae-economy-set-to-grow-56-in-2026-central-bank-report-1.500501523); [CBUAE — Quarterly Economic Review, March 2026](https://www.centralbank.ae/media/lgnfakgc/qer-march_2026.pdf).*

## 5. What this means for UAE industrial businesses

Putting it together, UAE industrial firms face a **mixed but manageable** environment:

**Tailwinds:**
- Strong domestic growth and government fiscal strength (boosted by high oil prices) support continued investment in industrial and infrastructure projects.
- Stable, predictable interest rates — no surprise rate rises so far in 2026.
- Low local inflation keeps domestic cost pressures contained.

**Headwinds / risks to watch:**
- **Higher oil prices raise input costs** for energy- and petrochemical-intensive manufacturers, and lift shipping and insurance costs while the Strait of Hormuz remains tense.
- **Supply-chain disruption:** firms that import raw materials, machinery, medical supplies or food by sea could see delays and higher freight costs if the Hormuz situation drags on.
- **Interest-rate risk:** if today's US inflation data comes in hot, the Fed could tilt toward a rate hike on 29 July, which the UAE would follow — raising borrowing costs for businesses on floating-rate loans.

**Bottom line:** the near-term backdrop is supportive for UAE industry, but management teams should keep a close eye on (1) today's US inflation report, (2) the 29 July Fed decision, and (3) the evolving Strait of Hormuz situation, any of which could shift financing costs or supply-chain conditions quickly.
