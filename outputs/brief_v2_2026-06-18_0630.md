## Stakeholder Bulletin (Type C)

**What happened:** Two major developments on June 17, 2026 will shape borrowing costs and energy prices across the UAE economy for months to come. First, the US Federal Reserve held interest rates unchanged for the fifth consecutive time — but its internal projections now point toward a possible rate *increase* later this year, a significant change in direction from the gradual easing many businesses had been expecting. Second, the United States and Iran have reached an agreement that will allow Iranian oil to flow through the Strait of Hormuz again for the first time since the regional conflict began earlier this year. Iranian tankers are already moving.

**What it means for UAE businesses:** On interest rates: the CBUAE immediately maintained the UAE base rate at 3.65%, and EIBOR — the rate that determines the floating portion of most UAE business loans — remains around 3.80%. However, if the US raises rates later this year as its officials are now signalling, the CBUAE would follow within 48 hours, which would increase repayment costs for companies with variable-rate facilities. On energy: the reopening of the Strait removes the emergency freight cost premium that has been pushing up import prices since early 2026 — good news for food importers, manufacturers, and any business shipping goods through the Gulf. The flip side is that more Iranian oil on the market will likely push global oil prices lower over the next several months.

**What businesses should consider:** Companies carrying floating-rate debt above AED 3mn should model what a 25bps rate increase would mean for their annual cash flow — for a AED 5mn facility, it adds approximately AED 7,300 per year. Food-sector importers can expect some freight relief as Hormuz normalises, but should not lock in spot contracts immediately as the deal terms are still being finalised at the June 19 signing in Switzerland. Manufacturers sourcing inputs from the United States should be aware that US inflation remains well above the Federal Reserve's target, which will sustain upward pressure on imported goods costs.
