## Stakeholder Bulletin (Type C)

**What happened:** Iran announced the Strait of Hormuz was closed to shipping, but the US military says it has struck to keep the waterway open and disputes that the closure is real. Oil prices jumped about 4% on the initial announcement and have stayed near $79 a barrel since, without climbing further. The US Federal Reserve and the UAE Central Bank both remain on hold, with no rate changes this week.

**What it means for UAE businesses:** The Strait of Hormuz carries about a fifth of the world's oil and gas trade, so any prolonged disruption raises the risk of higher shipping costs and delays for goods that move by sea — including medical supplies, food, and electronics components the UAE imports in large volumes. At the same time, higher oil prices mean more government revenue, which can support continued investment in industrial and infrastructure projects. Borrowing costs on UAE dirham loans have not moved and remain tied to the Fed's current stance.

**What businesses should consider:** Companies dependent on sea imports — particularly in healthcare, food, and electronics — should review inventory buffers and alternative sourcing or shipping routes in case the standoff continues. Manufacturers using oil-linked feedstocks (plastics, chemicals) should expect modestly higher input costs at current oil prices. The US inflation report due July 14 is the next data point likely to move interest-rate expectations ahead of the Fed's July 29 decision.
