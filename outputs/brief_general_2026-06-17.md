# Daily Macro Intelligence Brief — Emirates Development Bank
**Date:** 17 June 2026
**Prepared by:** General Financial Analyst (Baseline)

---

## 1. US Federal Reserve — Rates on Hold, New Chair Takes the Stage

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark interest rate unchanged at **3.50%–3.75%** at today's FOMC meeting, in line with near-universal market expectations. The decision was unanimous, though Governor Stephen Miran dissented in favour of a 25 basis point cut — a signal that some policymakers are growing impatient with the prolonged hold.

Today marks **Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Federal Reserve Chair**, following Jerome Powell's departure. Markets watched his 2:30 PM ET press conference closely for any tonal shift on the inflation-versus-growth trade-off. Warsh is widely regarded as more hawkish than his predecessor.

The backdrop for the hold is a May CPI print of **4.2% year-on-year** — the highest reading since April 2023 — driven largely by a 23.5% surge in energy prices and persistent shelter costs. Core CPI at 2.9% YoY remains above target. With inflation re-accelerating, the Fed has little room to cut even as growth shows signs of softening.

**Outlook:** Most analysts expect rates to remain at current levels through the end of 2026. The first cut is not broadly expected until mid-2027, barring a significant deterioration in the labour market or a sharp reversal in energy prices.

---

## 2. UAE Central Bank (CBUAE) — Mirroring the Fed

The Central Bank of the UAE has maintained its **Base Rate at 3.65%**, consistent with its policy of tracking the Federal Reserve given the AED/USD peg. No change is expected following today's Fed hold.

The CBUAE published its March 2026 Quarterly Economic Review maintaining a **5.6% real GDP growth forecast for 2026** — one of the more optimistic projections among major institutions. The bank cited strong non-hydrocarbon sector performance, robust tourism receipts, and ongoing government investment as key supports.

UAE borrowing costs have remained elevated throughout 2025–2026 as the peg mechanic prevents independent monetary policy. Businesses with floating-rate debt continue to face financing costs meaningfully above pre-2022 levels.

## 3. Oil Markets — Sharp Sell-Off on Iran Deal Hopes

Crude prices have fallen sharply over the past week. **Brent crude trades at approximately $78.57–$79 per barrel** as of today, down more than 3% in the session and extending a losing streak that has pushed prices to their lowest level since early March.

**WTI crude sits near $75.50/barrel.**

The catalyst is a reported US-Iran memorandum of understanding that would allow tanker transits through the Strait of Hormuz to resume and re-open Iranian crude exports to international markets. If confirmed and implemented, this would add meaningful supply to a market that had been tightened by Middle East tensions that drove Brent above $100/barrel earlier in June.

The sell-off reflects markets pricing in a structural supply increase, not just a temporary easing of tension. However, implementation risk remains — past Iran deals have been delayed or reversed — and OPEC+ may respond with production cuts to defend price levels.

**For context:** The May 2026 OPEC Monthly Oil Market Report showed the OPEC Reference Basket averaging $114.55/barrel in May — today's Brent price represents a dramatic reversal from that level.

## 4. UAE Economic Conditions

**Growth:** Multiple institutions forecast solid UAE growth in 2026, though estimates vary. The CBUAE holds its 5.6% projection; the World Bank projects 5.0%; the IMF, citing regional conflict spillovers, has revised its estimate down to **3.1%**, with a recovery to 5.3% pencilled in for 2027. The divergence reflects different assumptions about how quickly Middle East tensions — and now the Iran deal — will normalise.

**Inflation:** UAE consumer prices remain well-behaved. Inflation is forecast at **1.6%–1.8% for 2026**, well below the Fed's 2% target and far below US levels. This insulates domestic purchasing power and keeps the cost of living stable for the workforce underpinning industrial expansion.

**Industrial sector:** The UAE's industrial contribution has reached **AED 200 billion**, representing a 70% increase from baseline levels, according to statements by Dr Sultan Al Jaber at the "Make it in the Emirates 2026" launch in May. This puts the country roughly two-thirds of the way toward its Operation 300bn target of AED 300bn by 2031.

Industrial exports stand at AED 262bn, with advanced industrial exports (higher-value goods) accounting for AED 92bn of that total. The government has opened AED 180bn in procurement opportunities for domestic manufacturers and established a AED 1bn National Industrial Resilience Fund.

**Capital markets:** UAE equity markets are expected to see 9–12 IPOs in 2026 across aviation, utilities, and logistics — a sign of investor confidence in the domestic growth story.

---

## 5. What This Means for UAE Industrial Businesses

**Financing costs stay elevated.** With the Fed on hold and the CBUAE mirroring that stance, floating-rate borrowing remains expensive. Businesses planning capital expenditure should factor in sustained financing costs at current levels through at least 2026, and potentially well into 2027 before cuts arrive.

**Oil-driven input costs are falling — for now.** The crude price drop benefits energy-intensive industries (chemicals, metals, plastics, food processing) through lower input costs. However, this relief may be temporary if the Iran deal stalls or OPEC+ responds with cuts. Businesses should consider locking in forward contracts on energy-linked inputs while prices are low.

**The government procurement pipeline is open.** The AED 180bn in procurement opportunities under Make it in the Emirates 2026 represents a significant demand stimulus for domestic manufacturers. Combined with the AED 1bn Resilience Fund, businesses in the industrial supply chain have a real window to scale capacity with government-backed demand.

**Revenue and fiscal uncertainty.** Lower oil prices reduce UAE government revenues, which could over time affect the pace of government-led industrial investment. However, the UAE's fiscal breakeven oil price is well below current levels, so near-term budget pressure is limited.

**Bottom line:** UAE industrial businesses face a mixed near-term environment — high borrowing costs but improving input costs, a strong domestic policy push, and some macro uncertainty from oil price volatility and the IMF's downward growth revision. The businesses best positioned are those securing government procurement contracts and managing interest-rate exposure proactively.
